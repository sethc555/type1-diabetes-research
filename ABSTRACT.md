## Abstract

We build a within-host model of the type 1 diabetes (T1D) immune attack, grown layer by layer to answer a
connected set of immunotherapy questions, and then **calibrated to trial data and validated out-of-sample**.
The body spans the full disease arc — an innate trigger (IFN-α/neutrophils) that primes a self-amplifying
β-cell-stress→neoantigen→adaptive-attack loop, epitope spreading to new antigens, a B-cell/antibody arm, and
a metabolic readout in C-peptide and glucose — with genetics (HLA) setting the boundary conditions. The
*same* framework reproduces or predicts roughly eight independent real-world datasets/trials: it reconciles
the apparently-contradictory Foster 2025 (co-dosing antagonism) and Mathieu 2023 (safe combination) results
via a platform axis; reproduces the teplizumab response biomarkers; predicts checkpoint-inhibitor-induced
T1D; and **explains why several immunotherapy trials failed** — anti-IL-1 (given too late), single-antigen
tolerance (escaped by spreading), and anti-CD20 (transient B-cell repopulation). Calibrating the
load-bearing parameters to TN10, TEDDY/Ziegler, Shields/PROTECT, and Pescovitz reveals a **two-clock,
accelerating disease** and yields cross-dataset consistency (the same ~0.6/yr late-disease rate from two
unrelated cohorts). In leave-one-trial-out validation the model predicts the **natural history of trials it
was never fit to within ~2%**, while honestly bounding cross-stage drug-effect extrapolation as unreliable.
It produces one decision-relevant, re-analysis-testable prediction: stratifying teplizumab by baseline CD8
exhaustion should raise the response rate from ~56% (unselected) to ~91% (exhaustion-high). Every headline
number is machine-verified (19 checks). Illustrative within-host modeling / hypothesis generation — not
validated clinical findings, not a cure.
